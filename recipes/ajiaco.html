Ajiaco

Description

Ajiaco is a traditional Colombian soup from the mountains around Bogota. It's a hearty, thick soup defined by potatoes that break down during the boiling process and the dried guascas that give it a unique flavor.

Ingredients

  • Bone-in Chicken Breast(May replace with boneless chicken breast and bone-in chicken thigh)
  • Papas Criollas
  • Russet Potatoes
  • Red Potatoes
  • Corn on the Cob
  • Yellow Onions
  • Green Onions
  • Bay Leaves
  • Cilantro
  • Dried Guascas
  • Green Onions
  • Table Cream
  • Capers
  • Salt
  • Pepper
  • Sazon Todo
  • Chicken Bouillion
  • Garlic Powder
  • Onion Powder
  1. Marinade the chicken overnight with a good amount of salt, pepper, the garlic and onion powders, the sazon todo, and soy sauce. I like to add culantro, annato, and smoked paprika to this marinade also, but it's optional.
  2. Sear the chicken under medium to high heat in the pot you're going to use for the soup. This'll release fat and marrow from the bone and just make the chicken taste better. The chicken doesn't have to be cooked through at this stage; we're just searing for flavor now.When The skin is starting to look good, add the chopped onions so they can cook in the chicken fat until they smell good and start to look broken down.
  3. Fill the pot around 3/4 of the way with water and add the bay leaves, potatoes, and guascas at this point. Bring to a boil under high heat, and then leave at a simmer until the chicken is cooked through. Take the chicken out when it's cooked to let cool while the potatoes keep cooking and eventually breaking down.
  4. When the chicken is cool enough to handle, tear it with either your fingers or a fork. When the soup is in a state where it's decently thickened but there are still some large potato chunks in there add the ears of corn. Season the soup to taste with the spices mentioned earlier and the chicken bouillion.
  5. Add shredded chicken and cilantro to individual bowls and serve. May also add table cream to each individual bowl to taste.
